📚 Kickout Metrics Explained

Everything you need to understand what the dashboard is measuring and why it matters. Tap any card to read the full explanation. Use the search box to find a specific term.

🔍
🟢 Our Kickout KPIs
Own Kickout Win % KPI
The percentage of MKL's own kickouts where we win the first ball. This is the most fundamental kickout metric — if you don't win the restart, everything else is irrelevant.
Example: If MKL takes 16 kickouts and wins 12 of them, the Own KO Win % is 75%.
🎯 Target: 75%+
2-Phase Retention % KPI
Of the kickouts we win, how many do we still have possession of after two or more phases of play? A "phase" is one sequence of possession — a carry, pass, tackle won, etc. Winning the first ball is only step one. If we immediately turn it over, the kickout was wasted. 2-Phase Retention shows whether we actually secure the restart and build from it.
Example: We win 12 kickouts. 7 lead to controlled possession of 4+ actions. 2-Phase Retention = 58%. The other 5 were turned over quickly.
🎯 Target: 55%+
Positive Attack % KPI
Of the kickouts we win, what percentage result in a score or a foul won? This is the ultimate measure of how well we convert kickout possession into genuine attacking threat. It skips all the middle phases and asks: did winning this kickout actually help us score?
Example: We win 22 kickouts. 9 end in a score or foul won. Positive Attack % = 41%.
🎯 Target: 50%+
Clean Possession % KPI
Of the kickouts we win, how many were a clean uncontested catch with no real challenge from the opposition? Clean possession means the first receiver had time and space to make a decision. Contested catches and breaking balls are won but they are messy — harder to build from and more likely to result in turnovers.
Example: We win 22 kickouts. 18 are clean catches or no-contest situations. Clean Possession % = 82%.
🎯 Target: 75%+
Immediate Turnover % Warning
Of the kickouts we win, how many do we lose within 0–1 actions? This is the worst possible outcome after winning a kickout — we get the ball and immediately give it back. This usually happens because the first receiver is under too much pressure or makes a poor decision. Keeping this low is critical.
Example: We win 22 kickouts. 5 are turned over within one action. Immediate TO % = 23% — too high.
🎯 Target: below 15%
🔴 Opposition Kickout KPIs
Opp Turnover % KPI
The percentage of opposition kickouts that MKL wins. When the opposition takes a kickout, our job is to win the ball or force a poor restart. A high Opp Turnover % means our press is working and we are disrupting their restarts.
Example: Opposition takes 32 kickouts. We win 13 of them. Opp Turnover % = 41%.
🎯 Target: 40%+
Scores Conceded Rate Warning
Of the opposition kickouts they successfully retain, what percentage end in them scoring? This measures how dangerous the opposition is once they win their restart. Even if we can't win the ball, we want to ensure the defence is organised so retained kickouts don't lead to scores.
Example: Opp retains 19 kickouts and scores from 3 of them. Score Conceded Rate = 16%.
📏 Kickout Length
Short Kickout Field Term
A kickout played to a target close to the 20m line or shorter. Short kickouts are designed to keep possession by finding an unmarked player quickly. They carry the lowest risk because there is little or no contest for the ball. The downside is limited field gain — the team must then work the ball upfield from deep.
MKL data: Short kickouts win at 100% — the safest option available, especially under pressure.
Mid Kickout Field Term
A kickout played to the midfield area, typically targeting a midfielder or half forward around the centre of the pitch. Mid kickouts offer a balance between field position gained and risk taken. They require a clear runner to win the contest.
MKL data: Mid kickouts win at 71% overall — solid, but zone matters. Z4 wins at 100%, Z6 under press drops to 50%.
Long Kickout High Risk
A kickout played beyond midfield, targeting a half forward, full forward or contested aerial ball deep in opposition territory. Long kickouts can win big field position if successful, but they carry the highest risk of turnover. They should only be used when an overload or clear target is set up in advance.
MKL data: Long kickouts win at just 22%. Under heavy press: 0%. Avoid as a default option.

Zone Volume vs Efficiency Chart
The dual zone chart shows two things at once for each zone: how many kickouts were played there (the bar length) and what percentage were won (the coloured dot). This prevents a common mistake — overvaluing a zone with a 100% win rate from just one or two kickouts. Both volume and success rate matter.
Example: Z7 shows 2 kicks at 50% win rate — LOW sample tag. Z4 shows 7 kicks at 100% — HIGH sample, genuinely reliable.
💪 Pressure & Press
Heavy Press Field Term
The opposition is actively pressing the intended kickout receiver before the ball arrives. The receiver is marked and under pressure to win the contest or make a quick decision. Heavy press situations are the most difficult for the kickout taker — the risk of losing the ball is significantly higher.
MKL data: Under heavy press we win just 25% of kickouts. Under no press: 100%. The call and the receiver choice become critical under heavy press.
Press Success by Opp Length Chart
When the opposition takes a kickout, how often do we win it depending on how long their kick is? This tells us where our press is working. If we win 67% of their mid-range kickouts but 0% of their short kickouts, we know we need to press their short receiver harder.
MKL data: Opp short = 0% turnover. Opp mid = 67% turnover. Opp long = 33%. Target Z6 and midfield areas for best press return.
Scenario Matrix Chart
A grid showing the win percentage for every combination of pressure level and kickout length. It answers the question: given what the opposition is doing to us right now, what is the safest kickout to play? Green cells are high win %, red are low. This is designed to be used as a live decision tool during matches.
Example reading: Heavy press + Long = 0%. Heavy press + Short = 100%. Under any press, short is always the call.
⚽ Possession Chain
First Action After Win Field Term
What does the player who wins the kickout do with the ball first? The four options are Carry (solo or run), Handpass, Kickpass, or Foul Won (fouled in the act of winning). This matters because the first action sets the tone for the whole possession. Some first actions lead to far better outcomes than others.
MKL data: Carry leads to a positive outcome 64% of the time. Handpass leads to positive outcome just 12%. Carry first is the priority instruction.
Possession Flow Funnel Chart
A step-by-step view of what happens after we take a kickout: how many do we win, of those how many do we retain for 2+ phases, and of those how many result in a positive outcome. Each step narrows the funnel. The goal is to keep as many kickouts flowing all the way to a positive attack as possible.
Example: 34 kickouts taken → 22 won (65%) → 11 retained 2 phases (50%) → 9 positive outcomes (41%).
Attack Length Field Term
After winning a kickout, how many phases did the attack last before ending? Long Attack (6+ actions) means we built patiently. Short Attack (5 or fewer actions) means we scored or turned over quickly. Immediate (1–2 actions) means we went direct to goal or turned it over almost instantly.
Why it matters: Long attacks from kickouts show controlled, structured play. Immediate attacks can mean excellent direct play or panicked clearances — the resulting outcome tells you which.
Turnover Phase Warning
When we lose possession from a kickout-won attack, at what stage does the breakdown happen? Exit Pass means we lost it early trying to get out of our own half. Attack Build-Up means we lost it in the middle of the pitch building forward. Shot Attempt means we got a shot off but it went wrong. Knowing where we break down tells us exactly what to train.
Example: If most turnovers happen at Exit Pass, the problem is the first pass out of the kickout cluster. If it's Shot Attempt, the shooting decision or execution needs work.
🎲 Tactical Calls
Flat Four Tactical System
A kickout shape where four players set up in a flat horizontal line across the pitch to offer multiple short or mid-range outlet options. The aim is to give the goalkeeper a guaranteed uncontested pass by overloading one zone with options. The opposition has to mark across the whole width, which creates space somewhere.
MKL data: Flat Four = 100% win rate (4/4). All used on short kickouts. Most effective when the opposition spreads to cover all four outlets.
Quick Kickout Tactical System
Taking the kickout rapidly before the opposition can set their press or get players back. Speed of restart is the weapon here — the opposition is caught in transition and a runner who was behind the ball moments ago is now free. Requires excellent communication and awareness from the goalkeeper and the target player.
MKL data: Quick = 88% win rate (7/8). Works best on short and mid range. The one failure was a long quick kickout.
Overload Tactical System
Deliberately sending more players than the opposition has into a specific zone to create a numerical advantage. Rather than spreading out, the team floods one area so the goalkeeper always has a free player there. If the opposition shifts to cover the overload, it opens space elsewhere.
MKL data: Overload = 50% win rate (1/2) — small sample. Best used when opposition is zonal and not man-marking.
Long (Contested) High Risk
Deliberately playing a long kickout to contest an aerial ball deep in the opposition half. This is a high-risk, high-reward call. If won it provides instant field position. If lost the opposition have the ball in a strong position. Should only be used with a clear height or athleticism advantage over the target's marker.
MKL data: Long call = 0% win rate (0/2). Both lost under heavy press. This call needs an identified aerial target and a confirmed numerical advantage to be viable.
📊 Reading the Data
Sample Size Tags (HIGH / MED / LOW)
Every zone and tactical call shows a sample size tag. This is one of the most important things to understand when reading any sports data. A 100% win rate from 1 kickout tells you nothing. A 100% win rate from 10 kickouts is genuinely significant. The tags guide how much confidence to place in each number.

HIGH (5+ events) — Reliable. Enough data to act on.
MED (3–4 events) — Treat as a trend. Watch for more data.
LOW (1–2 events) — Interesting but not conclusive. Do not change tactics based on this alone.
Practical rule: Any metric with fewer than 3 events should be noted but not acted on until more data is available.
Benchmark Targets
The white vertical lines on the benchmark bars show the target threshold for each KPI. These are based on what elite LGFA kickout analysis suggests as the minimum standard for a team that wins more than it loses from restarts. Green means we are at or above target. Amber means close but not there. Red means below target and needs attention.
Targets: Own KO Win % → 75%+  •  2-Phase Retention → 55%+  •  Opp Turnover → 40%+  •  Positive Attack → 50%+
1st vs 2nd Half Trend
The Performance Trend section compares our kickout numbers across both halves of the same match. This reveals whether the team maintains kickout discipline as the game progresses or whether fatigue, tactical changes or opposition adjustments cause a drop-off. A strong 1st half and a poor 2nd half is one of the most common patterns in county-level LGFA analysis.
MKL pattern: 1st half consistently outperforms 2nd half across both matches. A simplified kickout menu in the 2nd half is recommended to protect the lead.
